Blackjack Etiquette

The unwritten rules that separate a welcome guest from the player everyone dreads. Learn them before you sit down.

Why Etiquette Matters

Casinos have cameras — the "eye in the sky" — that can see your hands but can't hear what you say. That's why blackjack has a specific set of hand signals and protocols. They're not just tradition; they're required for security and dispute resolution. Knowing proper etiquette also makes the game more enjoyable for everyone — including yourself.

Hand Signals

In face-up (shoe) games, you never touch the cards. You signal your decisions with hand gestures. In face-down (pitch) games, you pick up the cards with one hand and signal differently.

✋

Stand

Wave your hand horizontally over your cards (palm down, side to side).

👇

Hit

Scratch or tap the table surface behind your bet — an upside-down "come here" motion.

👉

Double Down

Place an additional bet equal to your original next to (not on top of) your chips. Point at your cards with one finger.

✌️

Split

Place an additional bet next to the original and make a peace sign/V-sign pointing at your cards.

↩️

Surrender

Swipe your index finger behind your bet (draw a line on the felt).

🛑

Bust

If you go over 21, simply place your cards face-up on the table. The dealer will scoop them.

Pitch Game (Face-Down) Signals: In face-down games, hold your cards in one hand. To hit, gently scrape the cards on the felt. To stand, tuck the cards face-down under your chips. Never use two hands — the casino sees it as a potential cheating signal.

Chip and Money Etiquette

✅ Do

Do stack your chips neatly, with highest denominations at the bottom and smallest on top.
Do place cash on the felt and say "Change, please" — the dealer will convert it to chips.
Do wait until the hand is over before touching your chips.
Do place double-down or split chips beside your original bet, not on top.
Do ask the dealer if you're unsure about a hand signal — they'll help.

❌ Don't

Don't touch your chips after the dealer says "No more bets."
Don't hand money directly to the dealer — place it on the felt.
Don't try to add chips to your bet mid-hand.
Don't pick up your chips before the dealer pays out the hand.
Don't throw chips onto the table — place them gently.

Tipping the Dealer

Dealers earn most of their income from tips (called "tokes"). Tipping is not required, but it's customary if you're winning. Here's the standard etiquette:

  • Bet for the dealer: Place a chip a couple of inches above your main bet. If you win, the dealer gets paid at the same odds as your bet.
  • Direct tip: Toss a chip toward the dealer or place it on the edge of the betting area between you and the dealer.
  • When leaving: Throw a chip into the center of the table as you color out.
  • How much: $1–$5 per winning hand is standard. Tip more on blackjacks and big double-down wins.

Note for card counters: Tipping gives back a portion of your edge. If you have a 1% edge and tip $5 on a $100 bet, you've just given back 5% — more than your entire edge. Counters should tip minimally or not at all.

Table Behavior — Do's and Don'ts

✅ Do

Wait for the dealer to invite you to join. Ask if players mind you joining mid-shoe. Use hand signals for every decision. Hold cards with one hand only. Play at a consistent, reasonable pace. Say "thank you" to the dealer after wins.

❌ Don't

Give unsolicited advice to other players. Criticize someone else's play (it's their money). Blame other players for "taking the dealer's bust card." Touch your phone during active play. Celebrate loudly or use abusive language. Accuse the dealer of cheating. Touch the cards in a face-up game.

The Third Base Responsibility

If you're sitting at "third base" (the last seat to the dealer's right, the last player to act), you have extra responsibility. Your decisions affect everyone because you could take a card that the dealer would have used. Here's what to do:

  • Play basic strategy without fail. Don't "try to help the table" by deviating.
  • Make your decisions promptly — the whole table is waiting on you.
  • Don't let other players pressure you into playing against basic strategy.

Drink Service

Casinos offer "free" drinks to players. But there are rules:

  • Always tip the cocktail waitress — at least $1 per drink, $5 on your first drink for faster service.
  • Don't get drunk. Impaired judgment + money = disaster.
  • Put down your drink away from the playing area — spills happen and can cause disputes.

Phone and Camera Use

In most casinos, phone use at the table is strictly limited. Never take photos or videos at a blackjack table — casinos prohibit it for security reasons. Keep your phone in your pocket during active play. Some casinos allow quiet phone use between shoes, but always check first.

Golden Rule: Be polite, use hand signals, tip when you win, and don't touch your chips during play. Follow these rules and you'll be welcome at any blackjack table in the world.

Play Responsibly: Good etiquette includes knowing when to leave. Set loss limits, respect the dealer and other players, and never gamble more than you can afford to lose. Learn more about responsible gaming.
