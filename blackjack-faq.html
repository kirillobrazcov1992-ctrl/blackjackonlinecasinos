Blackjack FAQ

Your most pressing blackjack questions answered — from basic rules to advanced strategy.

Rules & Gameplay
What's the objective of blackjack?
Beat the dealer's hand without going over 21. You don't need to get 21 — you just need a higher total than the dealer. If you go over 21 (bust), you lose automatically. If the dealer busts, you win regardless of your total. Full rules →
Are face cards worth 10 in blackjack?
Yes. Jacks, Queens, and Kings are all worth 10 points. Number cards (2–10) are worth their face value. Aces can count as either 1 or 11 — the choice is yours (and the computer/dealer will optimize it for you automatically).
What's the difference between a "hard" and "soft" hand?
A soft hand contains an Ace that can count as 11 without busting (e.g., Ace + 6 = soft 17). You can't bust by taking one more card. A hard hand either has no Ace or the Ace can only count as 1 (e.g., Ace + 6 + 7 = hard 14, because Ace as 11 would be 24 — bust). The distinction matters for strategy.
Do I win if I get 21 with more than 2 cards?
You win the hand (assuming the dealer doesn't also have 21), but it's not a blackjack. A "blackjack" specifically means 21 with your first two cards (an Ace + a 10-value card), and it pays 3:2. If you reach 21 with 3+ cards, it's just a regular 21 and pays even money (1:1).
Strategy & Odds
What's the best way to learn blackjack strategy?
Start by memorizing basic strategy — the mathematically optimal decision for every hand. You can print out a strategy chart and keep it next to you while playing. Practice with free online blackjack games. Most players pick it up in a few hours. After that, explore card counting if you want a deeper edge.
What's the house edge in blackjack?
With perfect basic strategy, the house edge ranges from ~0.17% (single deck, good rules) to ~0.67% (8 decks, standard rules). The average player who doesn't use basic strategy faces a house edge of 2–3%. The most important rule: never play 6:5 blackjack, which bumps the house edge to ~1.9%. See our odds page for details.
Should I take insurance?
Almost never. Insurance has a ~7.5% house edge — it's one of the worst bets in the casino. The only exception is if you're counting cards and the true count is +3 or higher, at which point insurance becomes profitable. More myths debunked →
Should I always split Aces and 8s?
Yes. Always split Aces and 8s, no matter what the dealer shows. A pair of Aces is worth 12 as a single hand (soft 12) but splitting them gives you two hands starting with 11 — very powerful. A pair of 8s is a terrible 16, but splitting gives you two shots at a decent hand. By contrast, never split 10s or 5s — 20 is too strong to break up, and 5s are better doubled.
Does the dealer have to hit on 16?
Yes. In all standard blackjack games, the dealer must hit on 16 or less and must stand on 17 or more. The dealer has no choices — their play is entirely dictated by the rules. Some games differ on whether the dealer hits or stands on soft 17 (H17 vs S17). S17 is slightly better for players.
Card Counting
Is card counting illegal?
No. Card counting is 100% legal. You're using your brain to process publicly available information — that's not cheating. However, casinos are private establishments and can refuse service to known card counters. They can ban you, but they can't have you arrested. Learn the Hi-Lo system →
Does card counting work online?
Generally, no. Most online blackjack games use Continuous Shuffling Machines (CSMs) that reshuffle after every hand, making card counting impossible. Live dealer blackjack with manual shuffles can sometimes be counted, depending on how often the dealer shuffles. But most live dealer games shuffle frequently (every 4–6 rounds) to prevent counting.
How much money can you make counting cards?
It depends on your bankroll and skill level. A card counter with a $10,000 bankroll playing $10 minimum tables might earn $10–$20 per hour on average — but with huge variance (losing weeks and winning weeks). Professional counters with $100,000+ bankrolls can earn $50–$100+ per hour, but it's a job with no guaranteed paycheck. Most people find card counting more valuable as a hobby that gives them a shot at winning than as a primary income source.

What's the difference between RNG and live dealer blackjack?
RNG blackjack is computer-simulated — the cards are dealt by software using a random number generator. It's fast and available 24/7. Live dealer blackjack streams a real table with a human dealer via HD video. You can see real cards being dealt and chat with the dealer. Bankroll & Money
How much money do I need to play blackjack?
You should bring at least 40–50 times the minimum bet as your session bankroll. For a $10 table, bring $400–$500. This gives you enough cushion to survive normal swings. Never play at a table where the minimum bet requires more than 2% of your session bankroll per hand. Complete bankroll guide →
What's a "push" in blackjack?
A push (or tie) is when your hand total equals the dealer's total. When this happens, nobody wins — your original bet is returned to you with no additional payout. Pushes happen about 8–9% of the time in standard blackjack.
Should I ever play 6:5 blackjack?
No. Never. 6:5 blackjack increases the house edge by about 1.4%, turning a ~0.5% edge into ~1.9%. Over 100 hours of play, this difference can cost you hundreds or thousands of dollars. If a casino only offers 6:5 blackjack, play at a different casino. See the math →
Is it better to play with fewer decks?
Yes — all else being equal. Single-deck blackjack has the lowest house edge (~0.17% with good rules). Eight-deck games have the highest (~0.67%). However, many single-deck games have poor rules (like 6:5 blackjack) that negate the advantage. Always compare the full rule set, not just the number of decks. Compare variations →
