Blackjack Odds & Probability

Blackjack is unique among casino games — it's the only game where the odds can shift from the house to the player. Here's the math behind it.

The House Edge Explained

Every casino game has a built-in mathematical advantage called the "house edge." It's the percentage of each bet the casino expects to keep over the long run. In a game with a 0.5% house edge, the casino expects to keep $0.50 for every $100 wagered — though in the short term, anything can happen.

Blackjack is unique because the house edge can be dramatically reduced by player skill. A player who plays by intuition might face a 2–3% house edge. A player using perfect basic strategy can reduce it to under 0.5%. And a card counter can actually flip the edge to the player's side.

Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance is what makes blackjack feel streaky. Even with a small house edge, the standard deviation per hand in blackjack is quite high — roughly 1.15 betting units. This means:

  • After 100 hands of $10 each: 68% of the time you'll be within ±$115 of expected value
  • After 1,000 hands: 68% of the time within ±$364
  • After 10,000 hands: 68% of the time within ±$1,150

This high variance is why bankroll management is so important. You can play perfect basic strategy and still lose money over 1,000 hands — that's not a failure of strategy, it's just variance.

Expected Value (EV) Calculations

Expected value tells you what you can expect to win or lose per bet over the long run. The formula:

EV = Bet Amount × House Edge × Number of Hands

Example: A $10 player playing perfect basic strategy (0.5% house edge) for 1,000 hands:

EV = $10 × 0.005 × 1,000 = −$50 (expected loss over 1,000 hands)

Compare that to a player who doesn't use basic strategy (2% effective house edge):

EV = $10 × 0.02 × 1,000 = −$200

That's a $150 difference — just for learning basic strategy. Multiply by a lifetime of play and the savings are enormous.

Risk of Ruin

Risk of ruin is the probability that you'll lose your entire bankroll before winning. For a basic strategy player with a 0.5% house edge:

  • 50× min bet bankroll: ~15% risk of ruin over 100 hours
  • 100× min bet bankroll: ~5% risk of ruin
  • 200× min bet bankroll: ~1% risk of ruin
  • 500× min bet bankroll: Negligible risk of ruin

For card counters with a 1% player edge, the risk of ruin is different because they're betting more when they have an edge. Proper Kelly betting keeps risk of ruin around 1–2% regardless of edge size.
