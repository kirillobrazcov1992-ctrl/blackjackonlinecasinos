Blackjack Glossary

Every blackjack term explained — from Action to Whale. Bookmark this page for quick reference.

A

Action
The total amount of money wagered by a player over a period of time.
Advantage Play
Playing techniques that give the player a mathematical edge over the casino, such as card counting.
Anchor
The player who sits in the last seat at the table (third base) and acts last before the dealer.

B

Back Counting
Watching the game without playing, counting cards until the deck becomes favorable before joining.
Bankroll
The total amount of money a player has set aside for gambling.
Basic Strategy
The mathematically optimal way to play every hand based on your cards and the dealer's up card.
Blackjack
An Ace and a 10-value card as the first two cards. Also called a "natural". Pays 3:2.
Bust
Going over 21. Any player or dealer who busts automatically loses.

C

Card Counting
A strategy where players track the ratio of high to low cards to determine when they have an advantage.
Cut Card
A colored plastic card used to cut the deck before play begins.
Cut
The action of splitting the deck before dealing.

D

Double Down
Doubling your original bet in exchange for receiving exactly one more card.
Down Card
See Hole Card.

E

Edge
The mathematical advantage held by either the casino or the player.
Exposure
The total amount of money at risk on a single hand or session.

F

Face Cards
Jack, Queen, and King. Each is worth 10 points.
First Base
The first seat at the table (to the dealer's left), the first player to act.
Flat Bet
Betting the same amount on every hand regardless of the count.

H

Hard Hand
A hand without an Ace, or where the Ace can only count as 1 (e.g., A + 7 = "hard 18").
Hit
To request another card from the dealer.
Hole Card
The dealer's face-down card. Dealt at the beginning of the round.
House Edge
The casino's built-in mathematical advantage. In blackjack, this is 0.5-2% depending on rules.

I

Insurance
A side bet when the dealer shows an Ace, paying 2:1 if the dealer has blackjack.

P

Push
A tie between the player and dealer. The bet is returned, neither wins.

S

Soft Hand
A hand containing an Ace that can count as 11 without busting (e.g., A + 6 = "soft 17").
Split
When you have two cards of the same value, you can split them into two separate hands.
Stand
To keep your current hand and end your turn.
Surrender
Forfeiting half your bet before the dealer checks for blackjack. Not all casinos offer this.

T

Third Base
The last seat at the table (to the dealer's right), the last player to act.
True Count
The running count divided by the number of remaining decks. Used in card counting.

G

Greek (Hoop)
Slang for dealer. Derived from "George" (a good tipper) and later evolved into "Greek" as casino slang.

L

Late Surrender
Forfeiting half your bet after the dealer checks for blackjack. Available in some casinos — reduces the house edge by ~0.08%.
Low Cards
Cards 2 through 6. In card counting, these are assigned a value of +1 in the Hi-Lo system.
Lucky Lucky
A popular side bet that pays based on your two cards plus the dealer's upcard totaling 19, 20, or 21.

M

Martingale
A betting system where you double your bet after every loss. Unsustainable in blackjack due to table limits and bankroll constraints.
Multi-Deck
A blackjack game played with 4, 6, or 8 decks shuffled together. Standard in most casinos. More decks = higher house edge.

R

Re-Split
Splitting a hand that was already split (e.g., splitting 8s and getting another 8). Most casinos allow re-splitting up to 4 hands.
Risk of Ruin
The probability that a player will lose their entire bankroll before winning. Serious players aim for less than 5%.
Running Count
The current total in a card counting system. Must be converted to a true count by dividing by the number of remaining decks.

W

Whale
A high-stakes player who bets very large amounts. Casinos compete aggressively for whale business with comps and perks.
Wonging
The practice of back-counting — watching a table without playing, then joining when the count becomes favorable. Named after Stanford Wong.
