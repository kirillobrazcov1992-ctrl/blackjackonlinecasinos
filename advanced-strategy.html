Advanced Blackjack Strategy

You've mastered basic strategy. You know the rules. Now it's time to go deeper — index plays, composition-dependent decisions, and advanced techniques that squeeze out every fraction of a percent.

Beyond Basic Strategy

Basic strategy gives you about 99% of optimal play. But there's a remaining 1% that separates a basic strategy player from an expert. That 1% comes from three areas: index plays (card counting deviations), composition-dependent strategy, and rule-specific adjustments.

This page assumes you already know basic strategy and card counting. These techniques build on both.

The Illustrious 18

There are hundreds of possible index plays — deviations from basic strategy based on the true count. But most of them add so little value that they're not worth memorizing. Don Schlesinger, in his book "Blackjack Attack," identified the 18 most valuable deviations, known as the Illustrious 18. These 18 plays capture nearly all the potential gain from strategy deviations.

Memorize in Order: Start with the top 5 (very high and high value plays) — they give you about 70% of the benefit. Add the medium-value plays next. Only bother with the low-value plays once you're comfortable with everything else.

Composition-Dependent Strategy

Basic strategy is based on your hand total and the dealer's upcard. Composition-dependent strategy goes a step further — it considers the specific cards that make up your hand. For example:

  • 16 vs. 10: Basic strategy says surrender when allowed, otherwise hit. But 16 made of 8-8 (split!) or 10-6 vs. 6-10 vs. 9-7 can yield different optimal plays in single-deck games.
  • 12 vs. 4: Basic strategy says stand. But a 12 made of 10-2 is slightly different from 7-5 or 6-6 (though the difference is small in multi-deck games).
  • Soft 18 vs. 2: Basic strategy says hit in H17 games, double in S17 games. But if your soft 18 is A-7, doubling is generally correct against dealer 2–6. If it's A-3-4 (after hitting), doubling is no longer possible.

In multi-deck games, composition-dependent plays add very little edge (~0.02%). In single-deck games, they're worth more (~0.1%). Most players don't need to bother with them unless they're playing primarily single-deck.

Betting Ramps

Your betting ramp — how much you bet at each true count level — is the most important factor in card counting profitability. A well-designed ramp balances profit against detection risk.

Example Betting Ramp (6-deck, $10 minimum)

True Count ≤ 0: $10 (1 unit)
True Count +1: $10–$20 (1–2 units)
True Count +2: $30–$40 (3–4 units)
True Count +3: $60–$80 (6–8 units)
True Count +4: $100–$120 (10–12 units)
True Count +5+: $120–$150 (12–15 units, if bankroll allows)

This 1–12 spread is standard for 6-deck games. For double-deck, a 1–6 spread is sufficient. For single-deck, 1–4 is enough.

A smooth ramp (gradually increasing bets) is less suspicious than a "jump" ramp (suddenly jumping from $10 to $100). Casinos train their staff to watch for jump bettors.

Wonging (Back-Counting)

"Wonging" — named after Stanford Wong — is the practice of standing behind a table and counting cards without playing. You only sit down and place bets when the true count becomes favorable (typically +2 or higher). When the count drops, you get up and leave.

Advantages: You never play at a disadvantage. Your effective edge can be 2–3× higher than a standard card counter.

Disadvantages: Casinos hate Wonging. They train their staff to spot "back-counters" and will ask you to leave or ban you. It's also not possible in most online games or crowded casinos.

Team Play

Team play was made famous by the MIT Blackjack Team, popularized in the book "Bringing Down the House" and the film "21." The classic model uses a "Big Player" system:

  • Spotters: Sit at tables and play minimum bets while counting cards. When the count becomes favorable (+2 or higher), they signal the Big Player.
  • The Big Player: Wanders around the casino, receives signals from spotters, and sits down to place large bets at hot tables.
  • Back-Counter: Watches from behind and signals the Big Player when the count is hot.

Team play allows for larger bets with lower risk (because the team shares bankroll) and reduces detection risk (because the same person isn't both counting and betting big). However, it requires discipline, trust, and a significant bankroll.

Wong Halves — An Alternative Counting System

The Hi-Lo system (covered on our card counting page) is the most popular system for a reason — it's simple and effective. But more complex systems can give you a slightly higher edge. Wong Halves, developed by Stanford Wong, assigns fractional values for better accuracy:

  • +1: 2, 7
  • +0.5: 3, 4, 6
  • 0: 5
  • −0.5: 8
  • −1: 9, 10, J, Q, K, A

Wong Halves is about 25% more accurate than Hi-Lo but significantly harder to use because of the fractional values. Most professional players stick with Hi-Lo — the extra accuracy isn't worth the mental strain for most people.

Tracking Your Results

Serious players track every session. Record:

  • Date and location
  • Rule set and deck count
  • Starting and ending bankroll
  • Hours played and hands per hour
  • True count distribution (how often you had an edge)
  • Win/loss by session

This data lets you calculate your actual edge, standard deviation, and whether you're playing profitably. If you're not tracking, you're guessing.
