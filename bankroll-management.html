Blackjack Bankroll Management

The single most underrated skill in blackjack. You can play perfect basic strategy, but without bankroll management, you'll still go broke.

Why Bankroll Management Matters

Most blackjack players focus entirely on strategy — learning basic strategy charts, card counting techniques, and game selection. But there's a dirty secret: even a card counter with a 1% edge can go bankrupt if they bet too aggressively. Bankroll management is the difference between a professional who plays for life and a gambler who's bust within a month.

The core principle is simple: you must size your bets so that normal variance doesn't wipe you out. Blackjack is a game of short-term luck overlaying long-term skill. A player with perfect basic strategy can easily lose 10, 20, or even 30 hands in a row. If your bets are too large relative to your bankroll, those losing streaks end your session — or your career.

The Math: Even with a 0.5% house edge (good basic strategy), the probability of losing 10 hands in a row in a standard 6-deck game is approximately 1 in 1,200. For a full-time player playing 50,000 hands a year, that streak will happen about 40 times per year. Are you bankrolled for it?

Total Bankroll vs. Session Bankroll

Before you sit down at a table, understand the two types of bankroll:

Total Bankroll

The entire amount of money you have set aside exclusively for blackjack. This is money you can afford to lose — it's not your rent, groceries, or savings. For serious players, this is kept separate from everyday finances, often in a dedicated account. Never gamble with money you can't afford to lose.

Session Bankroll

The amount you bring to a single session. A good rule of thumb: your session bankroll should be at least 40–50 times your minimum bet. For a $10 minimum table, bring $400–$500. This gives you enough cushion to survive normal downswings within a single session.

The Kelly Criterion

The Kelly Criterion is a mathematical formula developed by John L. Kelly Jr. in 1956 that tells you exactly how much of your bankroll to bet when you have an edge. It's used by everyone from professional blackjack players to Wall Street investors.

Kelly % = Edge ÷ Odds

For blackjack, the formula simplifies to: bet a percentage of your bankroll equal to your advantage. If you have a 1% edge, bet 1% of your bankroll. If you have a 2% edge, bet 2%. Most professional card counters use "Full Kelly" for maximum growth, or "Half Kelly" (half the recommended bet) for reduced risk.

In practice, Kelly betting means:

  • No edge (count neutral or negative): Bet the table minimum
  • 1% edge: Bet 1% of total bankroll
  • 2% edge: Bet 2% of total bankroll
  • Never exceed 5% of your bankroll on a single hand — even with a huge edge

Betting Systems — Which Work and Which Don't

Martingale System ✗ Does Not Work

Double your bet after every loss. In theory, you'll eventually win and recover all losses. In practice, you'll hit the table maximum, run out of money, or both. A losing streak of 7 in a row (not uncommon) forces you to bet 128× your original wager. This is a recipe for disaster.

Paroli System (Reverse Martingale) Neutral

Double your bet after every win. This lets you ride winning streaks but limits losses. While safer than Martingale, it doesn't change the underlying house edge. It's a betting pattern, not a winning strategy.

1-3-2-6 System Neutral

A betting progression popular among casual players. Bet 1 unit, then 3, then 2, then 6 on consecutive wins. If you lose at any point, reset to 1. It's a structured way to bet but doesn't overcome the house edge.

Kelly Betting ✓ The Only Mathematically Sound System

Unlike the systems above, Kelly betting is mathematically proven to maximize long-term growth while minimizing risk of ruin. But it requires knowing your edge — which means you need to be counting cards or playing with a known advantage.

Stop-Loss and Win Goals

Professional blackjack players don't just have betting strategies — they have exit strategies. Two simple rules will save you more money than any betting system:

Stop-Loss

Decide before you sit down how much you're willing to lose in a session. When you hit that number, walk away. No exceptions. A common stop-loss is 50% of your session bankroll — if you brought $500, you leave when you hit $250.

Win Goal

Less critical than stop-loss, but still useful. Set a realistic win target — roughly 25–50% of your session bankroll ($125–$250 on a $500 bankroll). When you hit it, cash out. You can always come back tomorrow.

Common Mistake: "I'll just play until I'm even." This is the most dangerous thought in gambling. "Even" is a moving target — you'll chase it all night and end up deeper in the hole. Set your limits before you play, not during.

Betting Spreads for Card Counters

If you're counting cards, your betting spread is your main tool for converting your counting edge into profit. The spread is the ratio between your maximum bet and your minimum bet.

  • Single deck: 1–4 spread is sufficient
  • Double deck: 1–6 spread recommended
  • 6-deck shoe: 1–12 spread is standard
  • 8-deck shoe: 1–15 spread may be needed

Your spread must be balanced against your bankroll. A 1–12 spread with a $10 minimum means your maximum bet is $120. With a $10,000 bankroll, that's 1.2% of your roll — very safe. With a $3,000 bankroll, it's 4% — dangerously high.

Common Bankroll Mistakes

  • Playing above your bankroll: Sitting at a $25 table with a $300 session bankroll is suicide. You can't survive the variance.
  • Chasing losses: Increasing bets after losses to "win it back" is emotionally driven and mathematically unsound.
  • No stop-loss: Playing until you either win big or lose everything. This guarantees eventual bankruptcy.
  • Over-betting a small edge: If you have a 0.5% edge, betting 5% of your bankroll is 10× the Kelly recommendation.
  • Tipping away your edge: If you're counting cards with a 1% edge, tipping $5 on a $100 win is giving back half your profit.

Play Responsibly: Bankroll management is about protecting yourself so you can enjoy the game long-term. Never gamble with money you can't afford to lose. Set limits, stick to them, and if you ever feel the urge to chase losses, take a break. Learn more about responsible gaming.

Quick Rules

  • Session bankroll = 40–50× min bet
  • Set stop-loss before playing
  • Never chase losses
  • Kelly bet your known edge
  • Never bet >5% per hand
