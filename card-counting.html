Card Counting — The Hi-Lo System

Card counting is legal, mathematical, and powerful. Master the Hi-Lo system and turn the odds in your favor — no photographic memory required.

What Is Card Counting?

Card counting is a mathematical strategy used in blackjack to track the ratio of high cards to low cards remaining in the deck. When the remaining deck is rich in high cards (10s, face cards, and Aces), the player has a statistical advantage because they're more likely to draw a blackjack or have the dealer bust. When the deck is rich in low cards, the dealer has the advantage.

Contrary to popular belief, card counting is not illegal — it's a mental skill, not a cheating device. However, casinos reserve the right to refuse service to known card counters, so discretion is a necessary part of the art.

Is Card Counting Legal? Yes. Card counting is 100% legal in all U.S. jurisdictions (except where local laws explicitly prohibit it). You're simply using your brain to process publicly available information. However, casinos are private establishments and can ban card counters at their discretion. The key is to be discreet and avoid detection.

The Hi-Lo System — Step by Step

The Hi-Lo system is the most widely used card counting method, popularized by Stanford Wong's "Professional Blackjack." It's simple enough to learn in an hour yet powerful enough to give players a real edge.

The Card Values

Card ValueCountCategory
2, 3, 4, 5, 6+1Low cards — more of these help the dealer
7, 8, 90Neutral — ignore these
10, J, Q, K, Ace−1High cards — more of these help the player

That's it. Six card values are +1, five are -1, and three are 0. Hi-Lo is famously simple because it doesn't need to be complex to be effective.

How It Works

1 Start at Zero

At the beginning of a fresh shoe (deck), your running count starts at 0. As each card is dealt and revealed, you add or subtract from the running count according to the Hi-Lo values above.

2 Maintain the Running Count

As the dealer reveals cards, you mentally track the count. For example: if the first round shows 3, K, 7, A, 8, 5 — your count goes: +1 (3), 0 (K), 0 (7), -1 (A), 0 (8), +1 (5) = running count of +1. You do this continuously for every card you see.

3 Convert to True Count

The running count alone isn't enough — you need to account for how many decks remain. In a 6-deck shoe, a running count of +6 means very different things at the start (where it's diluted across 6 decks) versus near the end (where it's concentrated).

True Count = Running Count ÷ Number of Remaining Decks

Example: Running count of +6 with 3 decks remaining → True Count of +2. Running count of +6 with 1 deck remaining → True Count of +6 (much stronger signal).

4 Adjust Your Bets

The true count tells you when to bet more. When the true count is 0 or negative — bet the minimum (the house has the edge). When the true count is positive — the player has the edge. Increase your bets proportionally: at true count +2, bet 2× your minimum; at +4, bet 4×; and so on.

5 Deviate from Basic Strategy

At high true counts, the mathematically optimal play changes. Known as "index plays" or "strategy deviations," these are small adjustments that squeeze out extra value. For example, at a true count of +3 or higher, you should take insurance — it becomes a winning bet. At +4, stand on 16 against a dealer 10 instead of hitting.

Betting Strategy

Your betting spread is the most important factor in card counting profitability. The idea is simple: bet small when the count is neutral or negative, bet big when the count is positive. A typical spread might look like:

  • True Count ≤ 0: 1 unit (minimum bet)
  • True Count +1: 1–2 units
  • True Count +2: 3–4 units
  • True Count +3: 6–8 units
  • True Count +4+: 10–12 units

A 1-to-12 spread is considered aggressive but is necessary for profitability in 6-deck games. In single-deck games, a 1-to-4 spread is sufficient.

Staying Undetected

Casinos train their staff to spot card counters. Here's what they look for, and how to avoid it:

  • Don't be obvious: Vary your bet sizes gradually. Don't jump from 1 unit to 12 units the moment the count turns positive.
  • Camouflage plays: Occasionally deviate from perfect play on small hands to look like a regular player.
  • Table hopping: Move between tables to avoid prolonged scrutiny from pit bosses.
  • Team play: The famous MIT Blackjack Team used a "Big Player" system — one person counts at a table, signals a teammate when the count is hot, and the teammate bets big.
  • Act natural: Chat with dealers, tip occasionally, pretend to be a tourist. The less you look like a "grinder," the less attention you'll attract.

Important: Card counting in online blackjack is generally not effective due to continuous shuffling machines (CSMs) that reshuffle after every hand. However, live dealer blackjack with manual shuffles can sometimes be counted, depending on shuffle frequency.

Recommended Reading

To dive deeper into card counting, these books are essential:

  • "Professional Blackjack" by Stanford Wong — The definitive guide to the Hi-Lo system.
  • "Beat the Dealer" by Edward O. Thorp — The original card counting classic.
  • "Blackjack Bluebook" by Fred Renzey — Accessible for beginners.
  • "The Big Book of Blackjack" by Arnold Snyder — Covers history, strategy, and advanced techniques.
