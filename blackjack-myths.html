Blackjack Myths & Misconceptions

Blackjack is surrounded by folklore, superstition, and bad advice. We separate fact from fiction — and show you what's actually costing you money.

Over the decades, blackjack has accumulated more myths than almost any other casino game. Some are harmless superstitions. Others actively cost players money. Here's the truth behind the most common misconceptions.

Myth: Insurance is a Good Bet

❌ MYTH

This is the most expensive myth in blackjack. Insurance is offered when the dealer shows an Ace — you can bet up to half your original wager that the dealer has blackjack. It pays 2:1.

Here's the problem: the dealer only has blackjack about 4 out of 13 times (≈31%). The insurance bet pays 2:1, which means it needs to win 33.3% of the time to break even. Since it wins only ~31% of the time, insurance has a house edge of about 7.5%. It's a separate, terrible bet that has nothing to do with your main hand.

The one exception: If you're counting cards and the true count is +3 or higher, insurance becomes mathematically profitable because the deck has an above-average concentration of 10-value cards.

Verdict: MYTH — Always decline insurance unless counting cards

Myth: Never Bust — Always Stand on 12–16 Against Anything

❌ MYTH

The "never bust" strategy — standing on all hands of 12 or higher — sounds logical (you can't lose if you don't bust, right?). Wrong. The math shows that standing on 12–16 against a dealer 7 or higher actually loses more money than hitting. Why? Because the dealer makes a hand (17–21) over 70% of the time when showing 7+. You need to try to improve your hand even if it means risking a bust.

Basic strategy tells you to hit 12–16 against dealer 7+ and stand 12–16 against dealer 2–6. Trust the math, not your fear of busting.

Verdict: MYTH — Stand against weak dealer upcards, hit against strong ones

Myth: Card Counting is Illegal

❌ MYTH

Card counting is 100% legal. You're using your brain to process publicly available information — that's not cheating by any legal definition. The famous 1979 New Jersey Supreme Court case (Uston v. Resorts International) established that casinos cannot use the legal system to ban card counters. However, casinos are private establishments and can refuse service to anyone. They can ask you to leave, ban you from the property, and share your photo with other casinos — but they cannot arrest you for card counting.

That said, using electronic devices, having accomplices signal you, or any form of collusion with the dealer is illegal. Counting cards with your brain? Perfectly legal.

Verdict: MYTH — Card counting is legal. Casinos can ban you, but they can't arrest you.

Myth: The Dealer is "Due" for a Bust

❌ MYTH

The gambler's fallacy — believing past events influence future independent outcomes — is one of the most pervasive and dangerous misconceptions in all of gambling. If the dealer has made 17, 18, 19, 20, or 21 for five straight hands, many players think "they're due for a bust." This is mathematically nonsensical.

Every hand is independent. The dealer's probability of busting with a 6 showing is always ~42%, regardless of what happened on the previous hand. The cards don't remember, the dealer doesn't "owe" you a win, and patterns you see are just random variance. There are no hot or cold tables — only short-term luck overlaying long-term probability.

Verdict: MYTH — The dealer is never "due" for anything

Myth: You Need a Photographic Memory to Count Cards

❌ MYTH

Hollywood has done a disservice here. Movies like "21" make card counting look like Rain Man, but the Hi-Lo system requires nothing more than basic arithmetic — adding and subtracting 1. The MIT Blackjack Team members weren't geniuses with photographic memories; they were regular students who practiced a simple counting system for a few months.

If you can add +1, 0, and -1 in your head while holding a conversation, you can count cards. The hard part isn't the counting — it's the bet variation, the discipline, the bankroll management, and avoiding casino detection.

Verdict: MYTH — Basic card counting is simple arithmetic anyone can learn

Myth: The Goal of Blackjack is to Get as Close to 21 as Possible

❌ MYTH

This sounds crazy — isn't that literally the objective? Actually, no. The real goal of blackjack is to beat the dealer's hand. There's a subtle but important difference. If the dealer has 18 and you have 17, you lose — even though 17 is "close to 21." If the dealer busts and you have 12, you win.

This distinction matters because it affects your decisions. You should sometimes stand on 12–16 not because you're close to 21, but because the dealer is likely to bust. And you should sometimes hit 12–16 not because you're far from 21, but because the dealer is likely to make a good hand. Play against the dealer, not toward 21.

Verdict: MYTH — The goal is to beat the dealer, not chase 21

Myth: 6:5 Blackjack is Just as Good as 3:2

❌ MYTH

"It's still blackjack — just pays slightly different." This is dangerously wrong. Switching from 3:2 to 6:5 blackjack payout increases the house edge by approximately 1.4%. That turns a game with a 0.5% house edge into a game with a 1.9% house edge — meaning you'll lose nearly four times as much money over time.

On a $100 bet, a 3:2 blackjack pays $150. A 6:5 blackjack pays $120. That $30 difference adds up fast. The general rule: never play 6:5 blackjack under any circumstances. It's simply not worth playing, regardless of any other favorable rules.

Verdict: MYTH — 6:5 nearly quadruples the house edge. Never play it.

Myth: Where You Sit Affects the Flow of Cards

❌ MYTH

Many players believe that "third base" (the last seat) is dangerous because you can "take the dealer's bust card." The idea is: if you hit when you shouldn't, you might take a card that would have made the dealer bust, causing everyone to lose.

This is superstition. First, you can't know which card the dealer would have gotten — the deck is random. Second, over the long run, the cards you take even out. Third, and most importantly, you should always play basic strategy regardless of what other players think. Play your hand optimally, ignore the superstitions, and let the math do its work.

Verdict: MYTH — Seat position doesn't affect the outcome. Play your own game.

Myth: Doubling Down is Too Risky

❌ MYTH

Doubling down means you double your bet in exchange for exactly one more card. Many novice players avoid it because they're afraid of risking twice as much. But doubling down on 9, 10, and 11 (against the right dealer upcards) is one of the most profitable plays in blackjack.

When basic strategy says double, you have a statistical advantage on that hand — meaning the expected value of doubling is higher than not doubling. Avoiding doubles "to play it safe" actually costs you money. Doubling down isn't risky — it's mathematically optimal.

Verdict: MYTH — Not doubling when the math says to is leaving money on the table

Myth: You Should Always Split 10s

❌ MYTH

20 is the second-strongest hand in blackjack — it wins about 83% of the time (depending on the dealer's upcard). Splitting 10s means you're risking twice as much on two hands that are each weaker than your original 20.

Basic strategy says: never split 10s. The one exception is in certain highly specific tournament situations where you need to bet big to catch up. In normal play, keep the 20 and smile.

Verdict: MYTH — 20 is a money-maker. Don't break it up.

The Bottom Line: Blackjack is a game of math, not magic. Every myth you unlearn puts money back in your pocket. Master basic strategy, manage your bankroll, and ignore the folklore.
