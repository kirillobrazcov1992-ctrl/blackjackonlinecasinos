Blackjack Side Bets Explained

Side bets look fun and can pay big — but they come with a hidden cost. Here's everything you need to know about insurance, Perfect Pairs, 21+3, and more.

Side bets are optional wagers offered alongside your main blackjack bet. They typically have high payouts — and even higher house edges. While the main game of blackjack has a house edge around 0.5%, most side bets have edges 5–20 times higher. Here's what you're up against.

Insurance Avoid

Side bet that the dealer has blackjack — pays 2:1

Insurance is offered when the dealer's face-up card is an Ace. You can place a side bet of up to half your original wager that the dealer has blackjack. If they do, insurance pays 2:1.

Why it's bad: The dealer has blackjack about 31% of the time when showing an Ace. For insurance to break even, it would need to win 33.3% of the time (2:1 payout = break-even at 1 in 3). The difference is the house edge of ~7.5%. Insurance is mathematically a losing bet for non-counters.

The exception: Card counters take insurance when the true count is +3 or higher, because the deck has an abnormally high concentration of 10-value cards, making the bet profitable.

House Edge: ~7.5%

Perfect Pairs High House Edge

Bet that your first two cards form a pair

Perfect Pairs pays if your first two cards are a pair of any kind. There are three tiers:

  • Perfect Pair: Same rank AND suit (e.g., two Aces of spades) — pays 25:1
  • Colored Pair: Same rank and color (e.g., two red Kings) — pays 12:1
  • Mixed Pair: Same rank, different colors (e.g., a red 5 and a black 5) — pays 6:1

The bet is tempting because pairs aren't that rare, but the pay tables are structured so the house keeps a significant edge. In 6-deck games with the standard pay table, the house edge is about 6.11%. Some pay tables are even worse.

House Edge: ~6–11%

21+3 Occasional Fun

Bet that your two cards + dealer's upcard form a poker hand

21+3 combines blackjack with three-card poker. Your two cards and the dealer's upcard are evaluated as a three-card poker hand:

  • Suited Trips: Three of a kind, same suit (e.g., three Kings of hearts) — 100:1
  • Straight Flush: Three cards in sequence, same suit — 40:1
  • Three of a Kind: Three of a kind, mixed suits — 30:1
  • Straight: Three cards in sequence, mixed suits — 10:1
  • Flush: Three cards, same suit — 5:1

Counterintuitively, more decks lower the house edge for this bet. In 8-deck games with a good pay table, the house edge can be as low as ~3.62%. In single-deck games, it's a terrifying ~13.4%.

House Edge: ~3.6–7.3% (best with 8 decks)

Lucky Lucky Best Side Bet

Bet that your two cards + dealer's upcard total 19, 20, or 21

Lucky Lucky is arguably the most player-friendly side bet in blackjack. It pays based on the combined total of your two cards and the dealer's upcard:

  • Suited 7-7-7: 200:1 (top payout)
  • Suited 6-7-8: 100:1
  • Unsuited 7-7-7: 50:1
  • Unsuited 6-7-8: 30:1
  • Suited 21: 15:1
  • 21 (any other): 3:1
  • 20: 2:1
  • 19: 1:1

With the best pay tables, Lucky Lucky's house edge is around 2.7% — still much higher than main-game blackjack, but significantly better than other side bets. Some double-deck versions can even be counted for a player advantage.

House Edge: ~2.7–5.3%

Lucky Ladies Steer Clear

Bet that your first two cards total 20

Lucky Ladies is a side bet that pays if your first two cards total 20. The more specific the 20, the bigger the payout — with the top prize being two Queens of hearts (the "Lucky Ladies").

The problem: 20 is a reasonably common hand, but the pay table is structured so that the house edge is extremely high — often exceeding 15% with standard pay tables. This is one of the worst bets you can make in a casino.

House Edge: ~8–17%

Bust It Entertainment Only

Bet that the dealer will bust

Bust It pays when the dealer goes over 21. The more cards the dealer uses to bust, the higher the payout. A 3-card bust pays 1:1, while an 8+ card bust (extremely rare) can pay 250:1.

On the surface, this seems appealing — the dealer busts ~28% of the time overall. But the payouts don't compensate for the frequency, and the house edge hovers around 5–10% depending on the pay table.

House Edge: ~5–10%

Should You Ever Play Side Bets?

The honest answer: rarely. Side bets are fun and can add excitement to the game, but they come at a steep price. Here's a practical approach:

  • If you're playing to win: Never play side bets. Period. The house edge is 5–20× higher than the main game.
  • If you're playing for entertainment: Set a fixed budget for side bets (e.g., $20 per session) and treat it as the cost of fun — the same way you'd pay for a drink or a show.
  • If you're counting cards: Only take insurance at true count +3+. Some side bets (Lucky Lucky) can be counted profitably in double-deck games, but this is advanced territory.

Warning: If you play side bets regularly, their high house edge will dominate your overall results. A $10 player who adds a $5 side bet to every hand increases their effective house edge from ~$0.05 per hand to ~$0.30–0.50 per hand. Over 1,000 hands, that's the difference between losing $50 and losing $300–500.

Play Responsibly: Side bets are designed to be entertaining, not profitable. Set limits, stick to them, and never chase losses on a side bet. Learn more about responsible gaming.
